Visualizing the Refugee Crisis in 200 Words or Less

By Johanna Chisholm

How I did it

In the wake of the Arab Spring, there have been significant changes to how the world looks today in comparison to how it did in the beginning of March in 2011. Dictators have been overthrown, regimes have been removed, and as an indirect consequence of all this change, there are more people displaced now than there were after WWII. While this story has been given a lot of attention within the media, particularly within the last year, the way the story has been covered has changed significantly since the early rumblings of March 2011.

To show this change, I decided to do a little digging into three different Western media outlets coverage of the refugee crisis: The New York Times, The Washington Post and the Guardian. I chose these three based on their circulation and readership, as they were consistently the top three most read publications during this time period. Next, I used Lexis Nexis to gather articles between March 2011, which is the date most experts mark as the beginning of the refugee crisis, and October 2016. The searches were broken down by year, beginning with March 1, 2011 to March 1, 2012 and so on. The next step was to choose a search equation that would generate enough hits to take a solid (at least 20 articles between 800 and 1200 words) sample, but would also be relevant to the discussion of the refugee crisis. I decided upon a rather simple equation: atleast10(refugee!) and then limit that search to the specified publication; for the purposese of this project I decided to stick to print rather than online articles, as it proved there was a lot of duplication using the latter.

Note: I do not argue that this is either a perfect or holistic analysis, though I was able to confirm that the articles that were selected using said search equation were both relevant and within the specified time period.

After gathering 20-22 articles for each publication for each year between 2011 and 2016, I copied them over into a text file and then ran each individual file through the TAPorAnalysis tool. This tool scans through text files and lists what the number of unique words (the number of times a different word is used), as well as breaking down the specific count for each word that was used in an article. For instance, in 2013, the most commonly used word for Washington Post articles with atleast 10 mentions of the word refugee in it was Syrian. For the New York Times it was camps.

Within the TAPor Analysis tool, you can choose to discard every day words, such as the, a, he, she, it, they, etc. This is called the Glasgow Stop List, and you can check it out hereto see what kinds of words were excluded from my search.

After gathering my data, I then used the individual counts of the words to guide the text size of the below word clouds.

A deeper analysis of the clouds is still required, but as you can already see taking shape within the clouds, the Guardian's coverage of refugees outside of the Middle East is more in-depth than that of the New York Times or the Washington Post. Words like 'Somalia', 'Kenya', 'Australians' and 'Timel' are seen much larger in the Guardian's cloud, and in some cases, they aren't even registering on the Post's or the Times' clouds.
